    values:
      - heading: How accurate is waterjet cutting? 
        body: Waterjet cutting achieves tolerances between ±0.003 inches (±0.076 mm) and ±0.005 inches (±0.127 mm) on most materials. High-precision waterjet systems can reach ±0.001 inches (±0.025 mm) under controlled conditions. Accuracy depends on material thickness, cutting speed, nozzle condition and machine calibration. Thin materials produce tighter tolerances than thick metals or composites.  

      - heading: How thick of material can a waterjet cut? 
        body: Industrial waterjet cutters can cut materials between 0.01 inches (0.25 mm) and 12 inches (305 mm) thick with high precision. Waterjets cut steel, aluminum, titanium, stone, glass, rubber and composites. Maximum cutting thickness depends on material density, pump pressure, abrasive flow rate and cutting speed. 

      - heading: What are the pros and cons of water jet cutting?
        body: Water jet cutting provides high precision, cold cutting and compatibility with metals and composites. Waterjets produce smooth edges without heat-affected zones or material warping. Water jet cutting operates slower than laser or plasma cutting on thin metals and increases operating costs through abrasive use, pump maintenance and high energy consumption. 

      - heading: Is water jet cutting better than laser cutting? 
        body: The main difference between waterjet cutting and laser cutting is that waterjets use high-pressure water with abrasives, while lasers use concentrated heat energy. Waterjet cutting performs better on thick materials, reflective metals and heat-sensitive parts because it avoids thermal distortion. Laser cutting performs better on thin metals by delivering faster speeds, tighter tolerances and lower production costs.

      - heading: What are the advantages of waterjet cutting? 
        body: Waterjet cutting provides high precision, cold cutting and compatibility with nearly all materials. Waterjets cut steel, aluminum and composites without creating heat-affected zones or material warping. Waterjet cutting produces smooth edges and handles thick materials up to 12 inches (305 mm) or more with specialized systems.

      - heading: What industries use waterjet cutting? 
        body: Waterjet cutting is used in aerospace, automotive, manufacturing, construction, mining, electronics, food processing and medical industries. Aerospace companies use waterjets to cut titanium and composites with high precision. Automotive manufacturers cut steel, aluminum and interior materials.

      - heading: Can waterjet-cut parts move directly into machining or fabrication?
        body: Yes. Waterjet cutting is often used as part of a larger manufacturing workflow. At A to Z Machine, waterjet-cut parts can move directly into machining, fabrication, welding or finishing operations within the same production environment. This helps reduce handling, improve consistency and simplify project coordination.
