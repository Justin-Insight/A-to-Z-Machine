    values:
      - heading: What does TIG stand for?
        body: TIG stands for Tungsten Inert Gas welding. It is also known as Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW). The process uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode and inert gas, usually argon, to shield the weld area from contamination during precision welding.
      - heading: What is TIG welding used for?
        body: TIG welding is used for precision welding of thin metals like stainless steel and aluminum. It is ideal for applications requiring clean, high-quality welds such as aerospace, automotive and food-grade equipment. TIG offers excellent control, making it suitable for detailed and aesthetic welds.
      - heading: What are the advantages of TIG welding?
        body: The advantages of TIG welding include precise control, high-quality welds and clean finishes with minimal spatter. It works well on thin metals and a wide range of materials like aluminum, stainless steel and titanium. TIG welding also allows for greater aesthetic detail and no filler when needed.
      - heading: What materials can be welded with TIG? 
        body: TIG welding can be used to weld stainless steel, aluminum, magnesium, copper, brass, titanium and chromoly. It performs best on thin metals and non-ferrous alloys where precision and clean welds are critical.
      - heading: What is the difference between TIG vs MIG welding?
        body: The main difference between TIG and MIG welding is the technique and material control. TIG welding uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode and offers high precision for thin or specialty metals. MIG welding uses a consumable wire electrode and is faster, making it ideal for thicker materials and production work.
